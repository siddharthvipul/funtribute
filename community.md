**Building Your Community Strategy**
--

This workbook is designed to guide you through the essential elements of creating a robust community strategy. Thoughtful consideration of these questions will help clarify your vision, identify your key audiences, and lay the groundwork for a sustainable and impactful community.

### **Part 1: Understanding Your Project and Audience**

The foundation of any successful community is a deep understanding of who it serves and what problems it solves. 
**1.1 Project Vision and Mission:**

* **What is the primary goal of your project?** What change do you want to see in the world? 
* **What is your project's mission?** In a sentence or two, what does your project do?

**1.2 Audience Segmentation:**

* **Who are the key groups of people you want to engage?** (e.g., end-users, developers, researchers, designers, partner organizations, etc.) 
* For each audience segment, consider the following:

| Audience Segment | What are their primary needs or pain points that your project addresses? | What are the biggest roadblocks preventing them from solving these problems now? | What unique value does your community offer them? |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| *Example: Educators* | *Need high-quality, free, and adaptable teaching materials.* | *Lack of time to create resources from scratch; difficulty finding relevant content.* | *Access to a curated library of peer-reviewed materials and a network for collaboration.* |

### **Part 2: The Community Experience**

This section focuses on where and how your community will gather and interact. 
**2.1 Community Platforms:**

* **Where will your community primarily gather for structured discussions and knowledge sharing?** (e.g., Discourse, GitHub Discussions, etc.) Why is this the right choice for your audience? 
* **What platforms will you use for real-time, informal communication?** (e.g., Slack, Discord, Zulip, etc.) How will this complement your primary platform?

**2.2 Onboarding and Initial Engagement:**

* **Who will be the first members you invite to your community?** (e.g., existing contributors, pilot users, partner organizations) 
* **What initial content and resources will you provide to make them feel welcome and guided?** (e.g., "How-To" guides, project setup instructions, welcome threads) 
* **How will you gather feedback on the initial onboarding experience?**

### **Part 3: Content and Engagement Strategy**

A healthy community thrives on consistent and valuable interaction. 
**3.1 Content and Conversation Starters:**

* **What regular content will you create to provide value and stimulate discussion?** (e.g., weekly updates, monthly newsletters, technical blog posts) 
* **What recurring conversation prompts or events can you host to encourage participation?** (e.g., "Ask Me Anything" sessions with the core team, "Show and Tell" Fridays, themed monthly discussions)

**3.2 Community Promotion:**

* **How will people discover your community?** List the key channels, newsletters, forums, and events where your target audience is already active. 
* **How will you frame your community as a core value of your project, not just an add-on?**

**3.3 Creating Contributor Pathways with "Good First Issues"**

A key part of your engagement strategy is creating clear, accessible pathways for community members to make their first contribution. A well-curated list of "good first issues" is one of the most effective ways to do this. This process connects directly to your public roadmap, turning strategic goals into actionable tasks for your community.

**A simple but powerful method for managing this is to evaluate tasks based on their Effort and Priority.**

* **Effort: How much work is required to complete the task?** This is often estimated using "T-shirt sizes": S (Small), M (Medium), L (Large).  
* **Priority: How important is this task to the project's goals?** This can be ranked as Low, Medium, or High.

By combining these two metrics, you can strategically decide what to work on and identify the perfect issues for new contributors.

**The Effort vs. Priority Matrix**

Use this matrix to categorize tasks from your roadmap or backlog.

| Category | Your Strategy & How to Label It |
| :---- | :---- |
| **High Priority & Small Effort** | These are your most valuable and accessible tasks. They provide immediate impact and are perfect for new contributors to achieve a quick, meaningful win. Action: These should be clearly defined and labeled good first issue or help wanted. These are the first tasks to encourage contributors to pick up. |
| **Low Priority & Small Effort** | These are also excellent good first issue candidates. They are fantastic for helping someone learn your project's contribution workflow (e.g., code style, testing, pull request process) without the pressure of a critical task. Action: Label these good first issue as well. They are a low-risk, high-value entry point for your community. |
| **High Priority & Large Effort** | These are your major project goals the significant features and epics that define your roadmap. They are too large for a single task and must be broken down. Action: Label these as epic or feature. The primary work here is to break them down into a series of smaller, more manageable issues (including some Small Effort tasks that can become good first issues). |
| **Low Priority & Large Effort** | These are long-term ideas or significant undertakings that are not currently a focus. They represent the work that will be attempted last, after more critical tasks are complete. Action: Label these someday-maybe or backlog. They should generally be avoided by new contributors as they require deep project knowledge and are not an immediate priority. |

**Your Plan for Contributor Pathways**

* **What Makes a Great "Good First Issue"?**  
  Beyond being small in effort, a good first issue should be:  
  * Self-contained: It doesn't require deep, systemic knowledge of the entire project.  
  * Well-documented: The issue description clearly explains the goal, the steps to reproduce the problem (if it's a bug), and a suggested path to the solution.  
  * Unclaimed: It's not already being worked on by a core team member.  
* **Action Plan:**  
  * **How will you consistently label issues with Effort and Priority in your issue tracker?**  
  * **Who on your team will be responsible for identifying, documenting, and labeling good first issues?**  
  * **Look at your issue tracker today. Can you identify and label 3-5 good first issues to create an initial pipeline for new contributors?**

### **Part 4: Measuring Success and Improving**

To ensure your community is healthy and growing, you need to track its progress and adapt your strategy.  
**4.1 Key Performance Indicators (KPIs):**

* **What quantitative metrics will you track to measure community health?** (e.g.,e.g., *Time to First Response* on issues, *Number of First-Time Contributors* per month, *Ratio of Active to Passive Members* on your platform)  
* **What qualitative feedback will you collect to understand the member experience?** (e.g., surveys on member satisfaction, interviews about their biggest challenges, time to first contribution etc)

**4.2 The RISE Model for Participation:**  
How will you **Recognize**, **Incentivize**, **Support**, and **Elevate** your community members?

| Category | Your Strategy |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Recognize** | *How will you publicly acknowledge valuable contributions? (e.g., contributor of the month, project showcases)* |
| **Incentivize** | *What tangible or intangible benefits will you offer for participation? (e.g., digital badges, access to beta features)* |
| **Support** | *How will you help new members get started and grow their skills? (e.g., mentorship programs, office hours)* |
| **Elevate** | *How will you provide opportunities for dedicated members to take on leadership roles? (e.g., co-authoring blog posts, speaking at events)* |

### **Part 6: Roles and Resources**

A successful community strategy requires dedicated people and resources to execute it. This final section helps you define who is responsible for the community and what practical resources are available to support them. Clarity on these points is crucial for turning your strategy into a reality.

**6.1 Community Leadership and Team Roles:**

Who will be the primary drivers of community health and engagement? Acknowledging these roles even if they are informal and volunteer-based is key to accountability and success.

| Role/Responsibility | Primary Owner(s) | Key Responsibilities |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Community Lead/Manager** | *Who is the main point of contact for community matters?* | *e.g., Setting strategy, moderating discussions, running events, onboarding new members.* |
| **Technical Support/Q\&A** | *Who will answer technical questions from the community?* | *e.g., Triaging issues, responding in forums/chat, reviewing first contributions.* |
| **Content Creation** | *Who is responsible for writing blog posts, newsletters, etc.?* | *e.g., Authoring technical tutorials, writing community updates, managing social media.* |
| **Event Coordination** | *Who will organize and run community events (online/offline)?* | *e.g., Scheduling "Ask Me Anything" sessions, hosting community calls, organizing meetups.* |

**6.2 Resource Allocation:**

What practical resources is the project committing to its community-building efforts? Being realistic about your available resources will help you scope your activities effectively.

* **Time Commitment:**  
  * On average, how many person-hours per week will your core team dedicate specifically to community engagement activities (as defined in 6.1)?  
  * Is this commitment formally recognized as part of contributors' roles, or is it a purely volunteer effort?  
* **Financial Budget:**  
  * Do you have a dedicated budget for community activities? (A "no" is a perfectly acceptable answer, but it's important to be clear.)  
  * If yes, what might this budget be used for? (e.g., platform subscription fees, virtual event software, digital badges, sending stickers/swag, sponsoring travel for speakers).
